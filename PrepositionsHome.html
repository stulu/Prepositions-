Defining Categories

Generally speaking, prepositions can be divided into eight categories: time, place, direction or movement, agency, instrument or device, reason or purpose, connection, and origin.

Many prepositions fall under a few categories so context is the key to working them out. The table below has some of the most common ones.

Category Preposition
Time at, in, on, for, during, since, by, until, before, after, to, past
Place at, in, on, by/near/close to, next to, beside, between, behind, in front of, above/over, below/under
Direction or Movement to, from, over, under, along, around, across, through, into, out of, toward(s), away from, onto, off, up, down
Agency by, with
Instrument or Device by, with, on
Reason or Purpose for, through, because of, on account of, from
Connection of, to, with
Origin from, of

Prepositions of time

A preposition of time describes when or for how long something occurred or will occur. The three most common prepositions of time are at, in, and on. Each preposition of time refers to a different increment of or point in time.

Preposition of time What it describes Examples
at specific times of days The meeting starts at noon or The film starts 2pm
in months, years, specific times of days Kids start school in February or In 2020 the world was in lockdown
on days and dates On friday let us all go for a drink or Chrsitmas day is on December 25th
for durations of time I have lived abroad for many years or The games last for 2 hours
during events happening at the same time You shouldn't use your phone during class time or We need pay rises during the year
since a continuous event originating from a specific point in the past I have been waiting her since 2pm or I have lived here since 2006
by a specific point in the future before which an event must be completed You must wake up by 6.30am to get the kids to school
until a continuous event that will end at a specific point in the future I can stay out until 2am or You can't leave school until 18 years old
before something before a specific time or event The sunset before it was time to or Kids will eat dessert before dinner if they could
after something following a specific time or event You should use your phone after class or The weekend happens after friday
to time in relation to the next hour of the day The clock says it is ten to four right now
past time in relation to the previous hour of the day The match should finish no later than half past 10

Prepositions of place

A preposition of place describes where something is located in reference to something else, or where something occurred or will occur. Like prepositions of time, the most common prepositions of place are also at, in, and on.

Preposition of place What it describes Examples
at specific points or locations I will be staying at the Hilton next week or Matias is at the bar buying drinks
in enclosed spaces The chcocolate is in the fridge or they work in the A&E department
on surfaces or tops of things Leave the keys on the table or the coat does not belong on the sofa
by, near, close to little distance away The carpark is by/near/close to the station or I live close to the city
next to, besides adjacency People do not like sitting next to/besides strangers or They live next to/besides the sea
between in the middle of 2 people or things I like the eggs between the burger and lettuce or She likes sitting between her 2 kids
behind something at the back of something I was stood behind so many people or The cats like to hide behind the sofa
in front of something situated before something Let's put the bed in front of the window or The car is in front of the house
above,over* something higher than something The plane flew over the houses too low or The spider was above the bed
below, under** something lower than something There is always a monster under the bed or The mark was below the required level so I didn't get in

(*Over and above both describe something higher than something else, but over can also describe something directly in contact with and covering something beneath it. For example: “I draped my jacket over the couch” is correct, whereas “I draped my jacket above the couch” is incorrect.) (**Under and below can both describe something lower than something else, but under can also describe something that is directly in contact with something on top of it. For example: “The puppy hid under the blanket” is correct, whereas “The puppy hid below the blanket” is incorrect.)


Prepositions of direction or movement

A preposition of direction or movement describes how, where, or in what way something moves.

Preposition of direction or movement What it describes Examples
to movement with a specific aim, direction, or destination The kids went to the cinema with friends
from movement with a specific point of origin The race started from London
over movement higher than and across something else The plane flew over city
above movement to a point higher than something else The UFO hovered above the London Eye
under/beneath movement lower than something The divers went under/beneath the sharks
along movement on a straight line or edge They all cycled along the path together
around movement in a circular direction I would run around the plaza to exercise
across movement from one end to the other To get to school quicker they would walk across a field that was private
through movement from one side of an enclosed space and out of the other Next year there will be a race through London instead of around
into movement ending inside something If you dive into the pool like that you could get hurt
out of movement ending outside something You have to get out of the sea when a storm is coming
toward(s)* movement closer to something As they raced toward the finishing line the pressure increased
away from movement farther from something The robbers tried to get away from the police but failed
onto movement ending on top of something You should be careful when getting onto the horse
off movement down or away from something Mind the gap when getting off the underground
up movement heading up If you look you can see the sun coming up
down movement heading down Super heros love to jump down from high places and land in style

(*Toward and towards are interchangeable. In formal American English, “toward” is preferred.)


Prepositions of agency

A preposition of agency describes a person or a thing that has caused or is causing something to occur. Sentences containing prepositions of agency are usually written in the passive voice and employ the prepositions by (for people) and with (for things). For example:

“The house was built by the three siblings.” (passive)

“The three siblings built the house.” (active)

“Her heart is filled with emotion.” (passive)

“Emotion filled her heart.” (active)


Prepositions of instrument or device

A preposition of instrument or device is used when describing certain technologies, machines, or devices.

These prepositions are by, with, and on. Typically, by refers to methods of transportation, whereas with and on describe the use of machines and other devices. For example:

“Aunt Patricia returned home by ferry.”

“She opened the locked door with an old key.”

“May I finish my homework on your computer?”


Prepositions of reason or purpose

A preposition of reason or purpose describes why something has occurred or will occur. Common prepositions of reason or purpose include for, through, because of, on account of, and from. For example:

“Everything I did was for you.”

“Through her bravery, we were able to escape unharmed.”

“Because of the traffic, I was late to the airport.”

“The employees refused to work on account of their low pay.”

“He knows from experience how to deal with them.”


Prepositions of connection

A preposition of connection describes possession, relationships, or accompaniment.

Of is used for possession, to for relationships between people or things, and with for accompaniment. For example:

“The Tower of London is located in London.”

“A well-written essay is impressive to Stuart.”

“Rosa wanted to go to the cinema with Juan.”


Origin

When we describe a person or thing’s origin (such as nationality, hometown/state, ethnicity, the place where something was built or designed, etc.), we typically use the preposition from (and, to a lesser degree, of).

For example:

“I met the most delightful couple from Argentina.”

“I’m from Essex originally, but I’ve lived in Buenos Aires for many years.”

“The new professor is of Russian descent.”

“The new computer from the tech giant should be revolutionary.”
